Facebook cover sizes and safe areas

A Facebook page cover displays at 820x312 pixels on desktop and 640x360 pixels on mobile, while a personal profile cover renders close to 820x312 with the profile picture overlapping the lower-left. Because the desktop and mobile crops differ, the reliable rule is to design large, center the content, and leave the edges quiet.

Here is how the main cover placements compare so you can plan a layout that survives every crop:

The practical takeaway: place your logo, headline, and any faces in the upper-right two-thirds of the banner, and treat the lower-left as a no-go zone. Designing at the larger upload size also gives Facebook room to downscale cleanly, which matters because the platform recompresses every cover on upload and a generous source resolution survives that pass with far less softening.

Business page vs personal profile covers

A business page cover and a personal profile cover serve different jobs and follow different layouts. A business cover sells — it carries a value proposition, brand colors, and often a call to action. A personal cover sets a mood — usually a photo or a simple graphic with little or no copy. The profile picture also overlaps a different spot, so each needs its own design.

  • Business page covers — include a short headline or value proposition, brand colors, and a clear call to action; keep copy in the upper-right so the page name and logo do not collide with it
  • Personal profile covers — favor a clean photo or abstract graphic with minimal text; the goal is atmosphere, not marketing
  • Seasonal and campaign covers — business pages swap covers for sales, launches, and holidays, while personal covers change far less often
  • Consistency — a business cover should match the page's posts and ads, which is easier when you reuse one palette and type style across everything

How do you keep a cover on brand

You keep a Facebook cover on brand by locking three things across every version: a fixed color palette, a consistent type feel, and a repeatable logo placement. When those stay constant, a viewer recognizes your page instantly even as the artwork changes for each season or campaign.

  1. Fix your palette — set your brand hex colors in the prompt so every banner uses the same family
  2. Lock the type feel — describe one font style (clean sans, bold display, elegant serif) and reuse it
  3. Repeat the logo spot — keep the logo in the same safe-area corner across covers
  4. Match your posts — echo the cover's colors and mood in your feed graphics
  5. Save a reference — keep your best cover as a style anchor for future generations

Choosing a visual direction is easier when you know the looks that read well as banners. A scan through the top AI art styles helps you pick a flat, gradient, or photographic treatment that suits a Facebook cover and stays legible at banner scale.

What makes a strong cover prompt

A strong cover prompt names four things: the subject or scene, the mood, the exact text to render, and the brand colors. Vague prompts produce generic banners, while a specific prompt that calls out layout and palette gives the AI enough direction to place content inside the safe area on the first or second try.

  • Subject and scene — state what the banner shows, from an abstract gradient to a product or a workspace
  • Mood and energy — words like calm, bold, premium, or playful steer the whole composition
  • Exact text — write the headline or tagline verbatim so the AI renders the right words
  • Colors and contrast — name your palette and ask for high contrast where text overlays the art
  • Layout cue — request that key elements sit upper-right to respect the avatar overlap

What mistakes cut off cover content

Most cover problems come from ignoring the two crops: content placed in the lower-left vanishes behind the profile picture, and content pushed to the left or right edges disappears on mobile. Low-resolution uploads and oversized text are the other common faults that make a banner look unprofessional.

  • Lower-left content — the profile picture overlaps this corner, so logos and text placed there get hidden
  • Edge-to-edge layout — the mobile crop is narrower than desktop, so anything near the side edges is cut off
  • Low-resolution upload — banners below the recommended size look soft and blocky on retina screens
  • Oversized or cramped text — copy that fills the canvas leaves no breathing room and reads poorly at small sizes
  • Mismatched colors — a cover that ignores your brand palette makes the page feel disconnected from your posts

The fix for all of these is the same habit: preview on both a phone and a desktop before you publish, and keep the essential content centered and clear of every edge. A quick test is to imagine the banner with the left third covered by a card — if the message still reads, the layout will survive the profile picture and the mobile crop. If it does not, move the text and logo right and regenerate before you commit the cover to your page.

When should you update your cover

Update a business cover for each major campaign, season, product launch, or promotion, which for active pages often means every four to eight weeks. A personal cover can stay put far longer. Because an AI maker turns a fresh banner around in minutes, updating for a sale or event is quick enough to keep the page current.

  1. Seasonal refresh — swap in a banner for the current season or holiday
  2. Product or feature launch — announce a new release with a cover that highlights it
  3. Promotions and sales — put the offer and dates on the banner during the campaign window
  4. Rebrand or palette change — refresh the cover the moment your brand colors shift
  5. Event countdowns — feature an upcoming webinar, opening, or launch date

Whichever trigger applies, keep the palette and logo placement constant so each new cover reads as the same brand wearing a fresh outfit rather than a different page entirely.
