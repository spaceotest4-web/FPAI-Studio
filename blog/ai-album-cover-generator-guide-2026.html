An AI album cover generator turns a short text prompt into finished cover art for your single, EP, or album — sized correctly for Spotify, Apple Music, and every other streaming service. Instead of hiring a designer or wrestling with layout software, you describe the mood, pick a style, and export a square image ready to upload with your release. On a phone, the whole job takes a few minutes.

This guide covers the exact specs streaming platforms expect, a prompt-to-export workflow in FP AI Studio, the visual styles that fit each genre, and the typography habits that keep your title readable when the cover shrinks to a playlist thumbnail.

The core idea: a cover is not a poster — it is a thumbnail. Most listeners first meet your music as a small square in a playlist, so contrast, a single focal point, and bold readable text matter far more than fine detail that disappears at that size.

What an AI album cover generator does

An AI album cover generator creates original square artwork from a written description, then lets you export it at the resolution streaming services require. It handles the visual concept — color, mood, subject, and style — so you can produce a release-ready cover without design software, stock photos, or a separate illustrator.

That single capability replaces three slower routes to a cover:

  • Commissioning a designer — useful for big releases, but slow and costly for a steady stream of singles
  • Hunting stock photos — generic, often licensed to dozens of other releases, and rarely a perfect fit
  • Learning layout software — a steep curve when all you need is one strong square image

The result is a cover you can iterate on quickly: generate several directions, compare them at thumbnail size, and refine the one that reads best before you commit.

What size should an album cover be?

Streaming cover art is a square 3000x3000 pixel image, and that single spec covers Spotify, Apple Music, and the major distributors. Export it in the RGB color space as a high-quality JPG or PNG, keep the design fully inside the square with no borders, and your cover will scale down cleanly to every thumbnail size.

The reasons behind the spec are practical. A 3000 px square is large enough to look sharp on a desktop display or a smart speaker screen, yet it compresses down to the tiny grid thumbnails in mobile apps without turning to mush. Sticking to one master file means you upload the same image everywhere instead of juggling per-platform sizes.

Aspect ratio1:1Every streaming service displays covers as squares

Generate your artwork at the full 3000 px square from the start. Upscaling a small image to meet the spec softens edges and text, so it is always better to design at the target size than to enlarge a low-resolution draft after the fact.

How do you make a cover with AI?

To make a cover in FP AI Studio, set a square 3000x3000 canvas, write a prompt describing the mood and style, generate a few options, then add your title and artist name as a text layer before exporting. The full workflow runs in a few minutes, and you can regenerate any direction that does not feel right.

  1. Set a square canvas at 3000 x 3000 px so the output matches streaming specs from the start
  2. Write your prompt — name the subject, mood, color palette, and visual style in one clear sentence
  3. Generate several options and compare them side by side rather than settling on the first result
  4. Pick a direction and regenerate or refine the prompt until the composition leaves room for text
  5. Add your text — overlay the artist name and title on a separate layer for crisp, controllable type
  6. Preview at thumbnail size to confirm the focal point and text still read when small
  7. Export the square as a high-quality JPG or PNG, ready to upload with your release

If your release needs more than one piece of art — a square cover plus a wide banner or a canvas for social posts — generate each in its own aspect ratio rather than stretching the square. Treat the cover as the hero image and build the rest of the set around its palette so the whole release looks unified.

Which style fits your genre?

Genre sets listener expectations, and a cover that matches those cues signals the sound before anyone presses play. Hip-hop leans bold and high-contrast, ambient and lo-fi favor soft gradients and grain, metal goes dark and textured, and pop runs bright and graphic. Start from your genre's visual language, then add a twist that makes the cover yours.

  • Hip-hop and rap — bold portraits, heavy contrast, gold or neon accents, confident large type
  • Lo-fi, ambient, and chill — muted gradients, film grain, hand-drawn or anime-style scenes, soft light
  • Electronic and house — geometric shapes, gradients, glitch and chrome effects, vivid color
  • Rock and metal — dark palettes, texture and grit, dramatic lighting, distressed or carved typefaces
  • Pop — bright flat color, clean graphic shapes, a clear central subject, modern sans-serif type
  • Indie and folk — natural light, illustration, vintage tones, understated and personal imagery

To go deeper on the look itself — painterly, cinematic, retro, minimal — browse the breakdown of the top AI art styles and pick the one that matches your sound, then weave that style name straight into your cover prompt.

What makes a strong cover prompt?

A strong cover prompt names four things: the subject, the mood, the color palette, and the visual style. Vague prompts like "cool album cover" give scattered results, while a specific sentence such as "a lone figure on a neon-lit rooftop at night, moody, deep purples and pinks, cinematic photography" gives the model a clear target to compose around.

Layer your prompt in this order so the model weights the most important elements first:

  1. Subject — the central image, whether a person, an object, a landscape, or an abstract form
  2. Mood — moody, energetic, dreamy, dark, playful; this steers lighting and color
  3. Color palette — name two or three colors so the cover stays cohesive and on-brand
  4. Style — photography, illustration, 3D render, collage, or a named art movement
  5. Composition note — "centered subject with empty space at the top" leaves room for your title

Resist the urge to ask the AI to render your title text inside the image — spelling and spacing are unreliable, and you will add type more cleanly afterward. How do you add the title and artist name?

Add your artist name and song title as a separate text layer after the artwork is generated, not inside the AI prompt. This keeps the type sharp at every size and gives you full control over font, placement, and color. Choose one or two readable typefaces, leave breathing room around them, and keep the text clear of busy areas.

A few habits keep cover typography legible from a desktop screen down to a tiny thumbnail:

  • Limit fonts — one display face for the title and one clean face for the artist name is plenty
  • Size for the thumbnail — if the title is unreadable at small scale, it is too small or too thin
  • Mind contrast — place light text on dark areas and dark text on light areas, or add a subtle shadow
  • Leave empty space — prompt for an uncluttered region so the type has somewhere clean to sit
  • Align deliberately — pick a clear position (centered, lower-left) and keep it consistent across a release series

The same generate-then-overlay approach works across visual formats. If you also design other release assets, the layered text habit carries over to the AI movie poster generator workflow, where titles and credits sit on their own layers for the same reasons.

Why design for the thumbnail first?

Most listeners first see your cover as a small square in a playlist, search result, or recommendation row — long before they ever view it full size. A cover that looks great large but muddy when shrunk loses attention at the exact moment it matters. Designing for the thumbnail means strong contrast, one clear focal point, and bold text from the start.

  • One focal point — a single subject reads instantly at small size; a crowded scene becomes noise
  • High contrast — strong light-versus-dark separation keeps the image legible when downscaled
  • Bold, simple text — thin fonts and fine detail vanish in a thumbnail, so go larger and heavier
  • Limited palette — two or three colors hold together better than a busy rainbow at small scale

The practical test is simple: shrink your finished cover to roughly the size of a thumbnail and look at it on your phone. If the subject and title still register at a glance, the cover will work in the feeds where listeners actually discover music.

Is a single cover different from an album cover?

No — singles, EPs, and full albums all use the same square 3000x3000 pixel artwork on streaming services. There is no separate single shape or size to design for. The difference is strategic, not technical: a single often carries a release on its own, while an album cover anchors a body of work and frequently sets the visual theme for several singles around it.

If you are rolling out several singles ahead of an album, build a reusable template in your generator: keep the palette, type placement, and style consistent, and change only the central subject for each track. That turns a string of releases into a recognizable visual series without redesigning from scratch each time.

Can you sell music with an AI cover?

In most cases yes, but the safe path is to check the terms of the tool you use and your distributor's content policy before you upload. Many platforms require that you hold the rights to the artwork, so avoid prompts that copy a living artist's style by name or recreate a trademarked logo, and keep your project files in case anyone asks how the cover was made.

  • Read the tool's license — confirm you are allowed to use the output commercially
  • Check the distributor's policy — content rules vary, and some flag obvious copyrighted material
  • Avoid named-artist mimicry — do not prompt for "in the style of [living artist]" for commercial covers
  • Skip trademarks — logos, brand marks, and recognizable characters can trigger takedowns
  • Keep your files — save prompts and exports so you can show how the artwork originated
