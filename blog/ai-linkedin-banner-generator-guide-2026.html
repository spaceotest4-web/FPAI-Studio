An AI LinkedIn banner is the wide header image behind your profile photo, and it is the first piece of visual real estate a recruiter, client, or connection sees. An AI LinkedIn banner generator builds that header from a text description — you choose an industry theme, place a short line of copy, and export a finished 1584x396 image without opening design software or hiring anyone.

This guide covers the exact banner dimensions, where the profile photo crops the image, the brush-to-export workflow in FP AI Studio, industry themes that read as professional, and the safe zones that keep your text visible on both desktop and mobile.

The core idea: a LinkedIn banner is not decoration, it is positioning. The 4:1 frame is short and wide, the profile photo eats the lower-left corner, and mobile crops the edges, so the design has to say one clear thing in the space that survives every layout.

What an AI LinkedIn banner generator does

An AI LinkedIn banner generator turns a written prompt into a finished header image sized for your profile. Instead of starting from a blank canvas, you describe the look you want — the theme, the colors, the mood — and the tool produces a 1584x396 background you can drop text onto and export, all from your phone.

That single capability replaces three slower routes to a header:

  • Stock photo hunting — no more scrolling marketplaces for a generic landscape that half-fits the frame
  • Template editors on desktop — the same result runs on Android in a few taps
  • Hiring a designer — produce a polished, on-brand header yourself in minutes

Because the background is generated to your description, it can carry your industry, your palette, and your personal brand rather than the same default scene thousands of other profiles already use. That originality is the quiet advantage of generating rather than picking from a gallery: the people who view your profile have never seen your exact header, so it registers as considered rather than borrowed. You also keep editorial control — change one word in the prompt and the mood shifts, which means iterating toward the right header costs seconds instead of another round of stock browsing.

What size is a LinkedIn banner?

A LinkedIn banner is 1584 by 396 pixels, a 4:1 ratio, and that is the resolution LinkedIn uses for the cover image on a personal profile. Designing at this exact size keeps the image sharp and prevents LinkedIn from stretching or cropping it when your profile loads on desktop or mobile.

Two practical limits go with that spec. The file should be a PNG or JPG, and LinkedIn accepts uploads up to 8MB, which is far more headroom than a banner needs. Generating at the native 1584x396 from the start avoids the soft, upscaled look that comes from stretching a smaller image to fill the frame.

  • Dimensions — 1584 x 396 pixels (4:1 aspect ratio)
  • File type — PNG or JPG
  • Max file size — 8MB on LinkedIn
  • Color — RGB, exported for screen rather than print

If you also build other profile assets at the same time, generating each at its native size is the same habit that keeps a professional AI headshot crisp inside its circular crop. It helps to picture the ratio before you start: 1584x396 is unusually wide and shallow, closer to a letterbox strip than the near-square images most photos are. Compositions that work in a normal frame often feel cramped here, so leave room horizontally and avoid stacking elements vertically where there is very little height to spare.

Where does the profile photo cover the banner?

Your circular profile photo sits over the lower-left corner of the banner on desktop, covering roughly the bottom 25 percent of the height and the left 15 percent of the width. Anything you place there — a face, a logo, a line of text — gets hidden behind the photo, so treat that corner as off-limits for important content.

The frame is not uniform across devices, which is the part that catches most people out. The desktop and mobile apps render the banner at different proportions and crop different amounts from the edges. The safe approach is to keep your key message in the upper-right two-thirds of the frame, where it survives every layout.

  1. Lower-left corner — reserved for the profile photo; leave it clean
  2. Left and bottom edges — trimmed on some mobile crops; keep them light
  3. Center band — generally visible, safe for supporting visuals
  4. Upper-right region — the most reliable place for your main text or tagline

When you generate the background in FP AI Studio, build it knowing the lower-left will be partly hidden — let the visual breathe there rather than placing the focal point in the one spot the photo will cover.

What theme fits your industry?

The right banner theme makes your field obvious at a glance and signals the kind of work you do. Finance and consulting lean clean and minimal, creative roles can be bolder and more illustrative, and tech sits in between with abstract gradients and geometric motifs. Match the theme to the expectations of the people you want to reach.

  • Finance, legal, and consulting — restrained palettes, deep blues and neutrals, clean geometry, generous whitespace that reads as trustworthy and precise.
  • Technology and engineering — abstract gradients, subtle grids, or circuitry motifs; modern and forward-looking without being noisy.
  • Creative and design — bolder color, texture, or illustrative elements that double as a sample of your taste and range.
  • Marketing and sales — energetic but on-brand color, with room for a value-proposition line in the safe zone.
  • Healthcare and education — calm, approachable tones, soft contrast, and clear space that feels reassuring rather than flashy.

If you want a sharper sense of which aesthetic direction suits you, the breakdown of top AI art styles is a useful reference for translating a vibe into a prompt the generator can act on.

Where should text go on a LinkedIn banner?

Text on a LinkedIn banner belongs in the upper-right two-thirds of the frame, kept to one short line in a font large enough to survive mobile scaling. Anything in the lower-left disappears behind the profile photo, and small or cluttered copy becomes unreadable on a phone, so restraint beats density every time.

The goal is a single message the eye catches in under a second: a value proposition, a specialty, or a tagline that adds to your headline rather than repeating it. Use these guidelines to keep the copy legible.

  1. One idea only — a tagline or value line, not a paragraph or a list of services
  2. Upper-right placement — clear of the profile photo and the trimmed edges
  3. Large, simple type — sized to read on a small screen, with strong contrast against the background
  4. Skip volatile details — leave out phone numbers and addresses that change and date the image
  5. Match your brand — reuse the colors and fonts you carry across your other assets

Carrying one palette and font across everything is what makes a profile feel deliberate. The same discipline applies when you build an AI business card — banner and card should look like two pieces of one identity.

Whichever route you choose, the deciding factor is rarely the tool and almost always the clarity of the idea behind the header.

What banner mistakes should you avoid?

The most common LinkedIn banner mistakes are using the wrong size, placing key content in the lower-left where the profile photo hides it, cramming in too much text, and uploading a low-resolution image that looks soft. Each one undercuts an otherwise strong profile, and each is easy to avoid once you know the frame.

  • Wrong dimensions — anything other than 1584x396 gets stretched or cropped and looks off
  • Content under the photo — faces, logos, or text in the lower-left corner vanish behind the avatar
  • Too much text — multiple lines and tiny type turn unreadable on mobile
  • Low resolution — an upscaled small image looks soft against a sharp profile
  • Brand mismatch — colors and fonts that clash with your other assets read as careless

When a banner is not landing, the fix is usually subtraction: simplify the composition, move the focal point out of the photo corner, and reduce the copy to one line against a clean background.
