The FP AI Studio image models — Flux, Mystic, Seedream V4.5, and HyperFlux — each render the same prompt differently, and picking the right one is the fastest way to get the result you pictured. One leans photoreal, one is tuned for lifelike detail, one favors bold artistic styles, and one trades a little polish for speed. Knowing which does what saves you a dozen wasted generations.

This guide breaks down what each of the FP AI Studio image models is best at, gives you a decision table organized by use case, and shows how to switch between them inside the app. The goal is simple: match the model to the job so your first generation lands close to your intent.

The core idea: there is no single best model — there is a best model for a given job. Photoreal, artistic, and fast are different targets, and the FP AI Studio image models are tuned for different ones. Choose by use case first, then refine the prompt.

The FP AI Studio image models at a glance

FP AI Studio ships four image models: Flux for balanced, realistic scenes; Mystic for high-fidelity photorealism; Seedream V4.5 for artistic and stylized work; and HyperFlux for fast iteration. Each interprets prompts differently, so the same words can produce four distinct images, and the right pick depends on whether you want realism, style, or speed.

At a high level, the four models cover three jobs most creators need:

  • Photoreal — Mystic first, Flux as a fast-realistic alternative
  • Artistic and stylized — Seedream V4.5 for illustration, concept art, and bold color
  • Speed and iteration — HyperFlux for quick drafts and composition tests

If you are new to prompting any of these, the AI image generation guide covers the fundamentals that apply across every model before you dive into picking one.

Why the model you pick matters

The model you choose shapes style, realism, detail, and how long a generation takes — often more than small wording changes in your prompt. A photoreal model will fight an illustrated look, and a speed-first model will not match a quality-first render pixel for pixel. Choosing well means fewer regenerations and a first result closer to your intent.

Three things change when you switch models on the same prompt:

  1. Style bias — some models default toward photographic realism, others toward illustration or painterly color
  2. Detail and fidelity — quality-first models resolve fine texture in skin, fabric, and foliage that faster models simplify
  3. Speed — a fast model returns drafts in a fraction of the time, at the cost of some final polish

Because each model reads a prompt through its own lens, the same phrasing can land very differently. That is why matching the model to the job comes before fine-tuning wording — and why a broader comparison of the best AI models is worth reading once you know your use case.

What is Flux best for?

Flux is the balanced all-rounder in FP AI Studio: it produces clean, realistic images with strong prompt accuracy and reliable composition. It is the model to reach for when you want a dependable result without tuning much — realistic scenes, everyday product shots, and general-purpose images where you value consistency over a specialized look.

Flux earns its place as the default because it rarely surprises you in a bad way. Prompts translate predictably, text and layout hold together, and the output reads as believable without heavy prompt engineering. Where it shines:

  • Realistic scenes — landscapes, interiors, and everyday settings that need to look plausible
  • Prompt accuracy — it follows detailed instructions closely, so complex prompts stay on target
  • General-purpose work — a safe first pick when you are unsure which model fits

A useful way to think about Flux is as the model with the fewest weaknesses. It will not out-render Mystic on a close-up portrait or out-style Seedream V4.5 on a poster, but it holds up across almost any subject you throw at it, which makes it the sensible starting point for a new project. When you are still deciding on style and composition, generating first on Flux gives you an honest, unstylized read of your prompt before you commit to a more specialized model.

If your scene needs the last measure of photographic realism — pore-level skin detail or convincing natural light on a face — step up to Mystic. For a deeper side-by-side of how these render, the Flux vs Mystic vs SDXL comparison lays out the differences prompt by prompt.

When should you use Mystic?

Use Mystic when photorealism is the priority. It renders skin, fabric, and natural light with fewer of the plastic, over-smoothed textures that expose AI images, which makes it the top pick for portraits, lifestyle scenes, and product photography. It trades a little speed for fidelity, so it is best on your final render rather than early drafts.

Mystic is the model you choose when the image has to pass as a real photograph. The gains show up most in the details people scrutinize:

  • Portraits — natural skin texture, believable eyes, and soft, directional light
  • Product and lifestyle — realistic materials and reflections for catalog-ready shots
  • Texture-heavy scenes — fabric, foliage, and surfaces that reveal a model's fidelity

The trade-off is time: a fidelity-first model takes longer per generation, so a common workflow is to draft compositions on a faster model, then run the winner through Mystic for the final image. Solid prompting helps here too — the AI prompt engineering tips guide covers the lighting and lens language that pushes photoreal results further.

What does Seedream V4.5 do best?

Seedream V4.5 is built for artistic and stylized output. It leans into illustration, concept art, poster design, and bold, saturated color, so it is the model to use when you want a distinct creative look rather than a literal photograph. For character designs, stylized scenes, and expressive pieces, it produces results the realism-first models will not.

Where the realistic models aim to disappear, Seedream V4.5 aims to have a point of view. That makes it a natural fit for creative and design work:

  • Illustration and concept art — painterly, graphic, and stylized rendering out of the box
  • Posters and key art — bold color and strong composition for eye-catching pieces
  • Character and world design — expressive, consistent styles for original characters and scenes

Seedream V4.5 also rewards more descriptive style language. Naming an art movement, a medium, or a color palette gives it clear direction, and it tends to commit to that direction more fully than the realism-first models would. That makes it a strong partner for mood boards, book and album covers, game art, and any project where a consistent visual identity matters more than literal accuracy.

If you feed it a prompt aimed at a photo, you will still get a stylized read on it, so reserve Seedream V4.5 for when style is the goal. When you need a literal, true-to-life result instead, Mystic or Flux is the better match.

When is HyperFlux the right call?

HyperFlux is the speed model in FP AI Studio. It is tuned to return a result in a fraction of the time of the quality-first models, which makes it ideal for iterating on prompts, testing compositions, and exploring variations before you commit. It trades some final detail for turnaround, so it works best as a drafting tool, not the finisher.

The value of HyperFlux is in how it changes your workflow. When each generation is quick, you can explore instead of guessing:

  1. Draft the composition — generate several quick variations to find the framing and layout you want
  2. Refine the prompt — adjust wording rapidly, seeing each change in seconds rather than a long wait
  3. Lock the direction — once a draft is right, switch to Flux or Mystic for the final high-detail render

Think of HyperFlux as your sketch pad and Flux or Mystic as the final canvas. Fast exploration up front usually produces a stronger finished image than laboring over a single slow generation. The habit is worth building: creators who iterate quickly tend to land on better final images because they have seen more of what a prompt can become before choosing one to polish.

How do you choose the model in FP AI Studio?

In FP AI Studio you pick the image model before you generate, then type your prompt and run it. Because the models share a prompt box, you can reuse the same prompt across all four and compare results, or switch models mid-project without starting over. Choosing the model first is the single biggest lever on your output.

A reliable workflow for getting the image you want:

  1. Decide the goal — photoreal, stylized, or a quick draft — before you write anything
  2. Pick the matching model — Mystic or Flux for realism, Seedream V4.5 for style, HyperFlux for speed
  3. Write a clear prompt — describe subject, setting, lighting, and style in plain terms
  4. Generate and compare — run the prompt, and if unsure, try it on a second model to see the difference
  5. Refine or finalize — tweak wording, or switch to a quality-first model for the final render

Minor prompt adjustments per model usually give the cleanest results, since each reads your words a little differently. If you want to go deeper on wording, the prompt engineering tips apply to every one of the FP AI Studio image models.
