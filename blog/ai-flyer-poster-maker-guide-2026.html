An AI flyer maker turns a short description of your event, business, or sale into a finished, print-ready layout in seconds — headline, supporting text, imagery, and color palette arranged for you instead of dragged into place by hand. On Android, you type what the flyer is for, generate a few options, edit the wording, and export at the right size for print or social. This guide explains how an AI flyer maker assembles a layout, walks through the describe-to-design workflow in FP AI Studio, and covers the one thing most first-time flyers get wrong: choosing the correct size for where the flyer will actually appear.

The core idea: a flyer is not decoration — it is one message delivered at a glance. The AI handles the arrangement, but you decide the single thing a reader should remember, and every choice of size, text, and image should serve that one message.

What an AI flyer maker does

An AI flyer maker generates a complete promotional layout from a text prompt, combining a headline, body copy, background imagery, and a color scheme into a balanced design. Instead of starting from a blank page or hunting through templates, you describe the event or offer and the tool arranges every element for you, ready to edit and export.

That single capability replaces three slower routes to a finished flyer:

  • Hiring a designer — no waiting on a brief, a draft, and a round of revisions for a simple notice
  • Wrestling with template editors — no manual resizing, font pairing, or alignment by hand
  • Settling for plain text — a generated layout looks designed rather than typed into a document

How an AI flyer maker builds a layout

An AI flyer maker reads your prompt for the subject, tone, and key details, then generates imagery and arranges text into a layout that follows basic design rules — visual hierarchy, contrast, and balance. It picks a focal point, sizes the headline above the body, and matches colors to the mood you described, all in a single pass you can then refine.

The pipeline behind one generated flyer looks like this:

  1. Parse the prompt — the tool extracts the event type, date, tone, and any text you want included
  2. Choose a layout — it selects a structure suited to the format, such as a poster grid or a top-heavy event flyer
  3. Generate imagery — a background or feature image is created to match the subject and mood
  4. Place the text — headline, details, and call to action are sized and positioned for hierarchy
  5. Apply a palette — colors and fonts are matched to the theme for a cohesive look

Two details decide quality at this stage: how specific your prompt is about the format and tone, and whether you give the tool the exact text you need on the flyer. A vague prompt produces a generic layout; naming the event, the audience, and the feeling you want gives the AI enough to make deliberate choices. For the imagery itself, the look you ask for matters as much as the words — browsing the top AI art styles helps you describe the visual direction you want.

Event, business, and sale flyers

The three most common flyers are event flyers, business or service flyers, and sale or promotion flyers. Each carries a different core message — a date, an offering, or a discount — and the AI flyer maker adapts the layout to lead with whichever matters most. Naming the type in your prompt produces a tighter result.

  • Event flyers — concerts, markets, classes, fundraisers. Lead with the event name and date, keep the venue and time close, and reserve the bottom for ticket or RSVP details.
  • Business and service flyers — a new shop, a service launch, an open day. Lead with the offering and a single clear benefit, then place contact details and location prominently so a reader can act.
  • Sale and promotion flyers — discounts, seasonal offers, grand openings. Lead with the offer in the largest type on the page, add the dates and any conditions, and finish with where to redeem it.

Whichever type you make, the rule holds: decide the one thing a reader must take away, give it the largest type and the strongest position, and let everything else support it. A flyer that tries to say five things equally says nothing clearly. For brand-led flyers, pairing the design with a consistent mark from an AI logo generator keeps your materials recognizable.

What size should a flyer or poster be?

For print handouts and noticeboards, A5 and letter are the standard flyer sizes, while A4 and larger work as posters seen from a distance. For online use, design at social dimensions instead so the flyer stays sharp on a phone. Matching the size to where the flyer appears is the single biggest factor in whether it reads well.

The table below covers the sizes you will reach for most, in both metric and US conventions:

Letter8.5 × 11 inUS handouts and flyers
A3297 × 420 mmLarge posters, shopfronts, hoardings

Social story1080 × 1920 pxStories and full-screen vertical

A practical rule of thumb: the closer a reader stands, the more text the flyer can carry. A handheld A5 leaflet can pack in details, while an A4 poster on a wall needs a short headline and a single image that reads from across the room.

Print versus social: which version do you need?

Print and social versions of a flyer are built differently. A print flyer needs a fixed paper size at 300 DPI and room for a bleed margin, while a social flyer needs screen dimensions, larger text, and a simpler layout. For most promotions you will want both — a print master and a resized social cut from the same design.

  1. Start with the print version — design at the correct paper size and export at 300 DPI for crisp printing
  2. Resize for social — recompose the layout to 1080 × 1350 px or 1080 × 1080 px rather than shrinking the print file
  3. Enlarge the text for screens — what is readable on paper is often too small in a scrolling feed
  4. Simplify the social layout — drop fine print and lead with the headline and one image
  5. Keep the message consistent — the same headline, colors, and call to action across both versions

The reason for two versions is that print and screen reward opposite habits. Print rewards detail and high resolution because the reader holds the page; social rewards bold, sparse layouts because the flyer competes with a fast feed. Designing one version and forcing it into the other role is where most flyers lose impact.

How do you lay out text on a flyer?

Good flyer text follows a clear hierarchy: one large headline, a smaller line of supporting detail, and a short call to action. Group related information, leave generous empty space, and limit yourself to two type sizes plus the headline. The reader should grasp the main message in two seconds before deciding to read more.

  • One headline — the largest element, carrying the single most important message
  • Supporting details — date, time, location, or offer, grouped together below the headline
  • A call to action — what to do next, such as a website, phone number, or RSVP line
  • Empty space — room around each block so nothing feels crammed
  • Restrained type — two or three sizes total, so hierarchy stays obvious

The AI flyer maker handles most of this arrangement automatically, but you control the words. Write the headline before you generate, keep it under about eight words, and resist the urge to fill empty space — the white around the text is what makes the message stand out.

How do you get a flyer that looks professional?

A professional-looking flyer comes down to three habits: write a specific prompt, proofread every detail, and export at the right resolution. Most amateur flyers fail not on design but on a typo in a date, an image that fights the text, or a blurry low-resolution print.

  1. Write a specific prompt — name the event, tone, and audience so the layout is deliberate, not generic
  2. Provide the exact text — type dates, prices, and contact lines so the AI places real content, not placeholders
  3. Keep contrast high — dark text on light areas, or light text over a darkened image, so it stays legible
  4. Proofread at full size — check spelling, dates, and phone numbers before anything goes to print
  5. Export for the destination — 300 DPI for print, screen dimensions for social
  6. Generate variations — compare two or three layouts and keep the one with the clearest hierarchy

When an AI flyer maker struggles

An AI flyer maker struggles with long blocks of small print, precise brand color matching, and rendering exact text inside generated imagery. The more a flyer depends on dense legal copy or pixel-perfect logo placement, the more hands-on editing it needs after the first generation.

  • Dense fine print — terms, schedules, and long lists belong in edited text layers, not generated art
  • Exact brand colors — a generated palette may sit close to your brand without matching it precisely
  • Text baked into images — words generated inside an image can come out garbled and should be added as editable text
  • Complex multi-page layouts — brochures and folded leaflets are beyond a single-page flyer tool

When a flyer needs more precision than one pass allows, treat the generation as a starting layout: keep the imagery and structure, then replace the text and fine-tune the colors by hand so the final piece matches your brand exactly.

FAQ

What size should an event flyer be?

For handouts and noticeboards, A5 (148 by 210 mm) is the standard event flyer size, with A4 (210 by 297 mm) for posters and window displays. In the US, letter (8.5 by 11 in) is the common equivalent. For a flyer you only post online, design at 1080 by 1350 px instead so it reads sharply on a phone screen.

What is the difference between a flyer and a poster?

A flyer is a small handout, usually A5 or letter size, meant to be held and read up close with detailed information. A poster is larger, A4 and up, designed to be seen from a distance on a wall or window. Posters carry less text and bolder visuals; flyers can pack in more detail because the reader is holding them.

Can I make both print and social media versions of one flyer?

Yes. Design the print version first at the correct paper size and 300 DPI, then export a separate social version resized to 1080 by 1350 px or 1080 by 1080 px. Keep the headline and key details, but enlarge the text and simplify the layout for the smaller screen so it stays readable in a feed.

Do AI-generated flyers print clearly?

They can, if you export at the right resolution. For print, you need 300 DPI at the final paper size, which means a high-resolution export rather than a screen-sized image. Generate the layout, confirm the export dimensions match your paper size, and add a small bleed margin if your print shop requires one before sending the file.
