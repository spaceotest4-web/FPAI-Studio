AI sky replacement swaps a flat, gray, or blown-out sky for a better one — a warm sunset, a clean blue, or a moody dramatic sky — and rebuilds the lighting so the new sky belongs to the scene. On Android, the whole edit now takes a couple of taps and a few seconds. There is no manual masking around tree branches and no desktop software to install.

This guide explains how AI sky replacement actually works, walks through the select-and-swap workflow in FP AI Studio, and covers the matching tricks that separate a believable sky from an obviously fake one.

The core idea: sky replacement is not just pasting a new sky on top. The fake look comes from light that does not match. The AI relights the foreground to agree with the new sky and feathers the horizon, which is why color and light matching matter more than the sky image you pick.

What AI sky replacement does

AI sky replacement detects the sky in your photo, removes it, and drops in a new one — then adjusts the foreground light to match. Unlike a simple paste, it follows the edge of every roofline, tree, and tower automatically and relights the scene, so the swapped sky reads as the sky the photo was actually shot under.

That single capability replaces three older editing chores:

  • Hand-masking the sky — no more tracing around branches and wires pixel by pixel
  • Desktop sky tools — the same result runs on your phone in seconds
  • Waiting for golden hour — give a midday shot a sunset instead of reshooting at dusk

How AI sky replacement works

AI sky replacement runs on two models working together: one segments the sky from everything else, and one relights the foreground to match the new sky you choose. The segmentation model finds the horizon and traces fine edges like leaves and antennas, while the relighting step shifts the foreground color and brightness toward the new sky.

The pipeline behind a single tap looks like this:

  1. Detect — the model identifies which pixels are sky, down to fine edges
  2. Mask — it builds a feathered selection along the horizon and object outlines
  3. Replace — your chosen sky is mapped into the masked region
  4. Relight — the foreground is tinted and rebalanced to match the new sky's light
  5. Blend — the horizon seam is softened so there is no hard cut-out edge

Two details decide quality at this stage: how cleanly the model separates fine edges like tree branches, and how well the relighting matches the foreground to the new sky. Because the relight is automatic, a sky shot at a similar time of day to your photo needs the least correction and looks the most natural.

It helps to understand what the relight is doing. When you drop a sunset behind a building shot at noon, the building was lit by hard overhead light, not warm low-angle light. The relight pass tints the foreground toward the sky's color and adjusts its brightness so the two halves agree. The closer the original light already is to the new sky, the less the model has to push — which is why matching time of day beats picking the prettiest sky in the picker.

Which sky should you choose?

The right sky depends on the time of day and mood of your photo. A clear blue sky suits bright daytime shots, a sunset adds warmth to landscapes and evening scenes, and a dramatic cloudy sky adds mood. The most believable choice is a sky shot at the same time of day and from a similar angle as your original.

  • Clear blue — best for daytime real estate and bright outdoor shots; clean and uncontroversial
  • Sunset and golden hour — warm, low-angle light for landscapes and lifestyle photos
  • Dramatic clouds — adds mood and depth to moody scenes; pair with a darker foreground
  • Soft overcast — replaces a blown-out white sky with gentle texture without changing the mood
  • Blue hour and twilight — cool, deep tones for dusk shots and evening exteriors

Whichever you pick, match the light direction first. If the sun in your foreground comes from the left, a sunset glowing on the right will look wrong no matter how good the sky image is.

How do you match the light and color?

Matching the light is what makes a sky replacement believable. Set the new sky's brightness so the horizon does not glow brighter than the foreground, tint the foreground toward the sky's color temperature, and keep shadow direction consistent. A warm sunset needs a warm foreground; a cool blue sky needs neutral-to-cool tones.

  1. Match the time of day — choose a sky photographed under light similar to your shot
  2. Check the light direction — the brightest part of the sky should sit where the sun was
  3. Warm or cool the foreground — let the relight push foreground color toward the sky
  4. Balance the brightness — a dim foreground under a bright sky reads as a paste-in
  5. Soften the horizon — a feathered seam beats a razor-sharp cut-out every time
  6. Relight the whole frame if needed — finish with AI relighting to lock the mood together

When the foreground and sky still feel disconnected, the fix is usually global, not local: a single relighting pass over the entire image ties the two halves into one coherent scene.

One quick test catches most mismatches before you export. Glance at the brightest edge of the new sky and the brightest edge of the foreground. In a real photo the sky is almost always brighter than the ground, but not by a jarring margin near the horizon. If the new sky blazes white while a building below it sits in shadow, lower the sky's brightness or lift the foreground until the transition across the horizon reads as a gradient rather than a hard line. That single check fixes the most common reason a swap looks pasted in.

Where is sky replacement used most?

The biggest use is real estate, where a blue sky over a listing photo makes a property look brighter and more appealing on a gray day. Landscape and travel photography come next, swapping flat skies for sunsets and dramatic clouds, followed by architecture, automotive, and event photos that need a cleaner backdrop.

  • Real estate listings — replace an overcast or white sky with clean blue so exteriors look their best. Pair with relighting to brighten shaded facades.
  • Landscape and travel — turn a flat midday sky into a sunset or dramatic cloudscape for more impact.
  • Architecture — a clean sky keeps the focus on the building's lines and removes a distracting haze.
  • Automotive and product — a controlled sky behind an outdoor vehicle shot looks intentional and premium.
  • Events and lifestyle — rescue an outdoor portrait shot under a gray sky with warm golden-hour light.

Real estate deserves a closer look because it is where sky replacement earns its keep. Listing photos are often shot on whatever day the agent is free, which means overcast skies and flat white backdrops are common. A clean blue sky behind the same house reads as a brighter, more cared-for property, and buyers browsing dozens of listings respond to that first impression. The key is restraint: a believable midday blue sells better than a fiery sunset that no buyer expects to see over a suburban home at a viewing.

Whichever you shoot, the workflow holds: detect the sky, choose a replacement that matches the time of day, then handle global edits like brightness and sharpening. The Android photo editor guide covers where each of these tools lives in the app.

Sky replacement vs relighting vs background swap

Sky replacement, relighting, and background swapping solve different problems and are easy to confuse. Sky replacement changes only the sky and matches the light to it; relighting changes the mood of the whole scene without replacing anything; background swapping replaces everything behind the subject. Pick the tool that matches what you want to change.

ToolWhat it doesBest for
Sky replacementSwaps the sky and relights the foreground to matchReal estate, landscapes, dull or white skies
RelightingChanges the lighting and mood of the whole sceneBrightening, time-of-day shifts, matching tones
Background swapReplaces everything behind the subjectPortraits, product shots, full scene changes

The three tools chain well together. A common sequence is to replace a gray sky with a sunset, then relight the foreground so its warmth matches the new sky, giving a result that looks shot at golden hour rather than edited after the fact.

When does AI sky replacement struggle?

AI sky replacement struggles with intricate edges like bare tree branches against bright sky, reflective surfaces such as glass buildings and water, and scenes with very little visible sky. The harder the edge is to separate, the more the model has to guess — and guessed edges are where halos and fringing appear.

  • Fine branches and wires — thin detail against bright sky can leave faint halos
  • Glass and reflective facades — buildings that mirror the old sky need a second pass
  • Water and wet surfaces — the reflection must be replaced to match the new sky
  • Very little sky — a sliver of sky gives the relight almost nothing to work from

When a swap is too ambitious for one pass, work in layers: replace the main sky first, then handle reflections in water or glass separately against the cleaner result you just created.

FAQ

Is AI sky replacement free to use?

FP AI Studio includes sky replacement in its Android photo editor, and most edits are free to produce. You select the sky, pick a replacement, and export the result. Free tiers across AI editors typically cap resolution or daily edits, so check the limit if you process large batches of listings or need full-resolution exports.

Will an AI sky replacement look fake?

Not if the new sky matches the scene. The fake look comes from a sky whose light direction, color, and brightness clash with the foreground. A good AI sky replacement relights the scene to match the new sky and feathers the horizon, so the edit reads as natural. Pick a sky shot at a similar time of day for the most believable result.

Is it legal to replace the sky in real estate photos?

Sky replacement is widely used and accepted in real estate marketing, but disclosure rules vary by region. Many listing services allow enhanced skies as long as the edit does not misrepresent the property itself. Replacing a gray sky with a blue one is generally fine; changing fixed features of the home is not. Check your local listing and advertising guidelines.

What is the best sky to use for a replacement?

The best sky is one shot at the same time of day and from a similar angle as your photo. A clear blue sky suits bright daytime real estate shots, a sunset adds warmth to landscapes, and a dramatic cloudy sky adds mood to moody scenes. Match the light direction so shadows in the foreground agree with the new sky.

Can I replace the sky and keep the reflection in water?

Yes, but it takes a second pass. Replace the main sky first, then select the water and apply a softened, slightly darker version of the same sky as a reflection. Real reflections are dimmer and less saturated than the sky above them. Matching them this way keeps a lake, sea, or wet road from giving the edit away.
