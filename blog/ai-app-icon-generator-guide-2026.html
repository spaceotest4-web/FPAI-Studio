An AI app icon generator turns a short description into a finished app icon — a clean symbol, the right colors, and a square master file you can drop straight into App Store Connect or Google Play. Instead of opening vector software and nudging anchor points for an afternoon, you describe the idea, generate a handful of options, and export the winner. This guide explains how an AI app icon generator works, lays out the exact pixel sizes and safe areas iOS and Android demand, and walks through a workflow in FP AI Studio that produces a store-ready icon instead of one that gets bounced in review.

The core idea: an app icon is read at the size of a thumbnail, not a poster. The icon you judge on a 1024-pixel canvas will live at 60 pixels on a home screen, so simplicity, contrast, and a single focal shape beat detail every single time.

What an AI app icon generator does

An AI app icon generator produces a complete, square icon from a text description, handling the symbol, palette, and composition in one pass. Unlike a generic image tool, it is tuned for the constraints icons live under: a tight square frame, heavy use at tiny sizes, and a focal point that survives being shrunk to a grid cell.

That single capability replaces three slower steps in the old icon pipeline:

  • Hiring out a one-off design — generate and iterate concepts yourself before committing budget
  • Vector software setup — no artboards, layers, or export presets to configure by hand
  • Manual resizing — start from one high-resolution master and scale to every store size

If you are also building out a wider brand identity, an icon often shares DNA with a wordmark, so it pairs naturally with the AI logo generator workflow for a consistent look across your app and store listing.

How AI icon generation works

AI icon generation runs on the same diffusion models that power text-to-image tools, guided by a prompt that describes the symbol, style, and colors you want. The model has learned the visual grammar of icons from millions of examples, so it centers the subject, balances the composition, and produces the flat or gradient finish modern icons use.

Behind a single tap, the pipeline looks like this:

  1. Prompt — you describe the concept, style, and color palette in plain language
  2. Generation — the model synthesizes several square options at once
  3. Composition — it centers the focal symbol and balances the negative space
  4. Refinement — you regenerate, adjust the prompt, or pick a favorite to iterate on
  5. Export — the chosen icon is rendered at high resolution as a square master

The prompt is where most of the quality comes from. A vague request like "app icon" returns generic results, while "minimal flat icon of a paper plane, teal-to-green gradient background, centered, no text" gives the model the focal shape, palette, and layout it needs. App icon sizes for iOS, Android, and the stores

Design your icon once at 1024x1024 pixels and export down from there. Apple takes a 1024x1024 marketing icon and generates the smaller sizes itself; Google Play wants a 512x512 listing icon plus on-device adaptive layers. Working from one large square master means every size stays sharp and consistent.

Here are the sizes that matter across both platforms and their stores:

App Store (iOS marketing)1024x1024 pxOpaque square, no rounded corners or alpha

The practical takeaway: never design at the small sizes. Build the artwork at 1024x1024, keep it a perfect square with a fully opaque background, and let each store and operating system crop or scale it. The understanding of composition that carries across these formats is the same one covered in the AI image generation guide.

What is the safe area, and why it matters

The safe area is the central region of an icon guaranteed to stay visible after the system masks it. On Android, an adaptive icon is 108x108 dp but only the center 66 dp is always shown, because the outer ring gets cropped into a circle, square, or squircle depending on the device. Keep your logo inside that zone.

This matters because Android does not display your icon as a fixed shape. The same artwork might appear as a circle on one phone, a rounded square on another, and a teardrop on a third. Anything you place in the corners is decorative and may be cut off, so the rule is simple:

  1. Center the focal symbol — keep your logo and any critical detail well inside the center 66 dp
  2. Treat corners as background — fill them with color or pattern, never logic-carrying detail
  3. Test against shapes — preview the icon as a circle, square, and squircle before exporting
  4. Avoid hard edges near the mask line — a logo touching the crop boundary looks clipped

On iOS the concern is different but related: Apple rounds your square icon with its own corner radius, so you must supply a full square with no rounded corners of your own. If you pre-round the corners, Apple rounds them again and the icon ends up with an awkward double bevel.

Why should an app icon be simple?

An app icon should be simple because it is displayed tiny and competes with dozens of others on a crowded home screen. At 60 pixels, fine detail collapses into mush, gradients band, and small text becomes unreadable. A single bold symbol with strong contrast reads instantly at any size, which is exactly what recognition depends on.

Simplicity is not a stylistic preference here — it is a function of how icons are actually viewed. A few principles keep an icon legible:

  • One focal element — a single symbol, letterform, or shape, not a busy scene
  • High contrast — the subject should pop against its background at thumbnail size
  • No text — the app name already appears as a label beneath the icon
  • Limited palette — two or three colors hold together better than a rainbow
  • Distinctive silhouette — the shape should be recognizable even in monochrome

What do the App Store and Play Store require?

Both stores require a square, fully opaque icon supplied at their marketing size — 1024x1024 for Apple, 512x512 for Google. Beyond that, each forbids platform UI, misleading badges, and promotional text in the icon. Meeting the format and content rules up front is what keeps an icon from being flagged during review.

The requirements both stores share, distilled:

  • Square and opaque — supply a flat square with no transparency and no rounded corners of your own
  • No platform imitation — do not mimic Apple or Google's own app icons or system glyphs
  • No promotional text — words like "free," "new," or pricing belong in the listing, not the icon
  • No fake UI — no notification badges, system buttons, or device frames baked into the artwork
  • Original artwork — you need the rights to every element in the icon

Apple applies its own corner mask, so the icon you upload should be a hard square; Android applies its adaptive mask, so the same square gets cropped into the device shape. Designing one clean opaque square satisfies both systems at once.

Which app icon mistakes get apps rejected?

The mistakes that get icons rejected are usually formatting and content issues, not artistic ones: transparency or non-square shapes, promotional text, fake notification badges, imitation of system apps, and pre-rounded corners. Each is easy to avoid once you know reviewers look for them, and fixing them rarely changes the design itself.

  • Transparency or alpha — a non-opaque icon shows checkerboard gaps and fails Apple's square requirement
  • Pre-rounded corners — supply a hard square; the platform rounds it, and doubling looks wrong
  • Text inside the icon — unreadable at small sizes and redundant with the app label
  • Fake badges or UI — a painted-on red notification dot is a common rejection trigger
  • Detail outside the safe area — a logo in the corners gets clipped by the adaptive mask
  • Imitating other apps — resembling a system or popular app's icon invites a takedown

When an icon comes back flagged, the fix is almost always mechanical: flatten the background to remove alpha, square the corners, strip any text, and pull critical detail toward the center. Regenerate with those constraints written into the prompt and the next version usually clears review.

FAQ

What size should an app icon be for the App Store and Play Store?

Design at 1024x1024 pixels as your master file. Apple requires a 1024x1024 marketing icon for App Store Connect and generates smaller sizes from it. Google Play requires a 512x512 high-resolution icon for the listing. From a single 1024x1024 square master, you can export every size both stores need, so always design large and scale down.

Should an app icon have text in it?

Avoid text in an app icon. At the small sizes an icon is actually displayed, words become unreadable and clutter the design. The app name already appears as a label beneath the icon on both iOS and Android. Use a single distinctive symbol or letterform instead, and let the icon do the recognition while the label handles the words.

What is the safe area for an Android adaptive icon?

An Android adaptive icon is 108x108 dp, but only the center 66 dp is guaranteed to be visible. The outer ring can be masked into circles, squares, or squircles depending on the device, so keep your logo and any critical detail inside that center safe zone. Treat the corners as decorative background that may be cropped.

Why was my app icon rejected from the App Store?

Common reasons are icons that mimic Apple's own apps, include pricing or promotional text, use the word free, show a transparent or non-square shape, or place a fake notification badge on the icon. Keep the icon a fully opaque 1024x1024 square with no rounded corners of your own, no platform UI, and original artwork, and most rejections disappear.
