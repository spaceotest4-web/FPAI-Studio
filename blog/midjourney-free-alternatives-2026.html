Midjourney makes beautiful images and charges for every one of them — the free trial is long gone, and plans start around ten dollars a month. The good news: the free side of AI image generation has caught up dramatically, and several no-cost tools now produce results most people cannot distinguish from paid output.

This list is honest about trade-offs. Midjourney still sets a benchmark for artistic polish, and each alternative below rations its free tier differently. Here are the seven that actually work, with the right pick for each kind of user.

The short answer: FP AI Studio is the strongest free all-rounder (and the only one that adds video); Bing is the simplest browser option; Leonardo suits style-controlled work; Stable Diffusion is unlimited if you can run it yourself.

Does Midjourney have a free plan?

No — Midjourney is paid-only. It discontinued free trials back in 2023 and has not brought them back; as of 2026, the cheapest subscription is roughly ten dollars per month. It also has no Android app, running instead through its web app and Discord. That combination — no free tier, no mobile app — is exactly why this list exists.

To be fair to Midjourney: its artistic quality and community remain excellent, and for professionals who generate constantly, the subscription can be worth it. This list is for everyone else — people who want genuinely good AI images at a price of zero.

What makes a real alternative

A "free alternative" only counts if you can actually create with it — not a five-image trial that dies on day one. Every tool on this list met four tests before inclusion.

  1. Genuinely free tier — usable indefinitely, not a trial with an expiry
  2. Real quality — output competitive with modern paid generators
  3. Practical access — reasonable sign-up friction and platform reach
  4. Honest limits — we name how each one rations, because they all do somehow

Every entry names what the tool is best at and where it falls short — because the right alternative depends on whether you create on a phone or desktop, occasionally or daily, stills or video.

1. FP AI Studio — best free all-rounder

FP AI Studio is a free Android app that goes further than replacing Midjourney — it covers ground Midjourney never touches. Image generation runs on six models (Mystic, Flux 2 Pro, Flux 2 Turbo, Flux Dev, Seedream V4.5, HyperFlux) with ratio, seed, and guidance controls. Then it adds what no other entry here has: ten AI video models including Kling V3 Pro with multi-shot scenes and motion control, plus an editing suite with 4x upscale, relight, background and object removal, and image expand.

The honest limits: the app is Android-only (a free no-signup web generator covers other devices), and there is no fine-tune training. But as a free replacement for a ten-dollar subscription — with video thrown in — it is the strongest overall package on this list. The full FP AI Studio review covers it in depth.

2. Bing Image Creator — simplest in a browser

Microsoft's free generator (inside Copilot and Designer) is the lowest-friction option here: sign in with a free Microsoft account, type a sentence, get images. It runs on OpenAI's models, which are exceptional at understanding casual, conversational prompts without any prompt-engineering skill.

The limits: stills only, no model choice or controls, conservative content filters, and a "boosts" system — generation is fast until your boosts run out, then slower until they refresh. It is the perfect occasional-use tool and the weakest heavy-use one. The FP AI Studio vs Bing comparison goes head-to-head in detail.

3. Leonardo AI — best for style control

Leonardo AI's free tier refills a daily token allowance, and what you spend it on is the platform's real draw: a large library of fine-tuned style models and image-guidance controls. For game assets, consistent styled sets, and repeatable aesthetics, it is the most Midjourney-like experience on this list — arguably deeper, since you pick the exact model style.

The limits: the daily allowance disappears fast in heavy sessions, an account is required, and it is a desktop-web product at heart. Casual users may find the interface more tool than they need. The FP AI Studio vs Leonardo comparison covers when each wins.

4. Canva Magic Media — best inside a design tool

If your generated image is destined for a social post, flyer, or deck, Canva's built-in generator saves a step: generate and lay out in the same editor, with templates and brand tools around it. The free plan includes a capped allowance of AI generations — fine for occasional use inside design work.

The limits: it is a design platform first, so model choice and generation control are thin, and heavy generators hit the cap quickly. Treat it as the convenience pick for designers rather than a dedicated generator — the FP AI Studio vs Canva comparison draws that line precisely.

5. Stable Diffusion — unlimited if you self-host

Stable Diffusion is the open-source route: run it on your own computer (via tools like ComfyUI or AUTOMATIC1111) and generation is truly unlimited, uncensored by a provider, and endlessly customizable with community models. Nothing else on this list matches that ceiling of control and volume.

The limits are the price of that freedom: you need a capable GPU, real setup effort, and patience for a technical learning curve. For non-technical users it is the wrong first stop — but for tinkerers with gaming hardware, it is the deepest free option in existence.

6. Ideogram — best for text in images

Ideogram earned its place with one standout skill: rendering readable, correctly-spelled text inside generated images — posters, logos with words, signage — where most models produce garbled lettering. It offers a free daily allowance and works in the browser.

The limits: outside its text specialty it is a solid but not exceptional generator, and the daily allowance rations heavy use. If your work involves words inside images, add it to your toolkit; if not, other entries serve you better.

7. Adobe Firefly — best for commercial caution

Adobe's generator ships a free plan with a monthly allowance of generative credits and one distinctive pitch: it is trained on Adobe Stock and licensed content, which Adobe positions as safer for commercial use. It also integrates with Photoshop and Express for editing workflows.

The limits: the monthly credit allowance is modest, the style range is more conservative than the artistic tools on this list, and the deepest value assumes you live in Adobe's ecosystem. It is the corporate-safe pick more than the creative-ceiling pick.

FAQ

Is Midjourney free?

No. Midjourney discontinued its free trial and now offers paid subscriptions only, with plans starting at roughly ten dollars per month as of 2026. If you want to generate AI images without paying, you need one of the free alternatives — several of which are genuinely capable rather than crippled trials.

What is the best free Midjourney alternative?

It depends on your platform and needs. FP AI Studio is the strongest free all-rounder on Android, adding video and editing that Midjourney lacks entirely. Bing Image Creator is the simplest free browser option. Leonardo AI suits style-controlled work within a daily free allowance, and Stable Diffusion is unlimited if you can run it locally.

Which free alternative is best on Android?

FP AI Studio — it is a native free Android app with six image models, ten video models including Kling motion control, and an AI editing suite. Midjourney has no Android app at all, so for phone-first creators the alternative is not just cheaper, it is more practical.

Are free AI image generators unlimited?

Most ration in some way — daily tokens (Leonardo), speed boosts (Bing), monthly credits (Adobe Firefly), or capped generations (Canva). FP AI Studio's app and web generator are free to use, and locally-run Stable Diffusion is truly unlimited but needs a capable GPU and setup effort. Check current limits before relying on any.

Do free alternatives match Midjourney's quality?

Midjourney remains a benchmark for artistic polish, and an honest list says so. But the gap has narrowed sharply — Flux-class models in FP AI Studio and OpenAI's models behind Bing produce results most viewers cannot tell apart from paid output, especially with a well-written prompt. For most real uses, free is now good enough.
