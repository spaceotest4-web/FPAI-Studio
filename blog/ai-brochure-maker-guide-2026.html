An AI brochure maker turns a short brief into a print-ready folded document — choosing the layout, placing imagery, and structuring the copy across each panel so you start from a finished draft instead of a blank page. With FP AI Studio you describe the fold type, the panels you need, and the topic, and the tool builds a tri-fold or bi-fold brochure you can refine in minutes rather than hours.

The core idea: a brochure is not one flat page — it is a sequence of panels the reader uncovers fold by fold. Plan the content around that reveal, and an AI brochure maker handles the layout, spacing, and balance for you.

Tri-fold vs bi-fold brochures

A bi-fold brochure has one fold and four panels, while a tri-fold has two folds and six panels. The bi-fold reads like a small magazine spread with room for large images, and the tri-fold breaks content into short, scannable blocks. Choose the fold by how much content you have and how you want the reader to move through it.

Fold typeFolds / panelsBest for
Bi-fold1 fold, 4 panelsLarger images, magazine-style spreads, fewer sections
Tri-fold2 folds, 6 panelsServices, steps, and pricing broken into blocks
Z-fold2 folds, 6 panelsSequential storytelling that unfolds left to right
Gate-fold2 folds, 6 panelsA dramatic reveal where both sides open to a center

The tri-fold and Z-fold use the same six panels but fold differently: a tri-fold tucks the right panel inward, while a Z-fold pleats like an accordion so the reader sees panels in strict order. For most business and event use, a tri-fold or bi-fold covers the job, and FP AI Studio defaults to those two unless you ask for an accordion or gate layout.

A simple way to decide: count your sections before you pick a fold. If you have two or three big ideas and strong imagery to show them off, a bi-fold gives each one room to breathe. If you have five or six short points — a list of services, a step-by-step process, or tiered pricing — a tri-fold splits them into tidy blocks that a reader can skim in seconds. Choosing the fold first also tells the AI brochure maker how to distribute your copy, so the draft comes back balanced rather than overflowing one panel and leaving another empty.

How to plan brochure panels

Plan brochure panels around the fold order, not left to right. On a tri-fold, the right panel folds in first and becomes the cover, the back panel holds contact details, and the inside three carry your main message. Sketch the fold, label each panel by what the reader sees in sequence, and assign one idea per panel.

For a standard tri-fold, the panel roles fall into a reliable order:

  1. Front cover (right outer panel) — headline, hero image, and the single promise that earns a pickup
  2. Inside left — the problem or context that frames why the reader should care
  3. Inside center — your core offer, services, or schedule, given the most visual weight
  4. Inside right — proof, details, or next steps that build confidence
  5. Back panel — contact details, address, and a clear call to action
  6. Inner flap (left outer panel) — a teaser, quote, or supporting visual seen as the cover folds open

The principle that holds across every fold: one idea per panel. Crowding two messages into a single panel forces the reader to work harder, and an AI brochure maker can only balance a layout cleanly when each panel has a clear, single job.

Brochure sizes and print specs

The most common brochure sizes are US Letter at 8.5 by 11 inches and A4 at 8.27 by 11.69 inches, both folded into bi-fold or tri-fold formats. For print, design at 300 DPI in CMYK color and add a 0.125 inch bleed on every edge. These specs keep type crisp and prevent thin white borders when the printer trims the sheet.

  • Resolution — 300 DPI for print so images and small type stay sharp on paper
  • Color mode — CMYK for print and RGB for any screen or PDF version
  • Bleed — extend backgrounds 0.125 inch past every trim edge
  • Safe margin — keep text at least 0.25 inch inside the fold and trim lines
  • Paper weight — heavier stock around 100 lb text holds a fold without curling

If you are exporting a digital-only brochure, you can drop the bleed and work in RGB, but keep the panel proportions identical so the file reads correctly when viewed as a multi-page spread. Always confirm the final trim size and bleed with your print shop before you export, since requirements vary by press.

One detail that trips up first-time designers is the difference between the flat sheet and the folded result. A tri-fold built on a Letter sheet measures 11 inches wide before folding, but the right panel is cut slightly narrower than the others so it tucks cleanly inside when the brochure closes. An AI brochure maker accounts for this offset automatically, but if you ever export panels to lay out by hand, remember that equal-width panels will not fold flat. Keeping the panel math handled for you is one of the quiet advantages of generating the layout rather than building it from scratch.

How do business and event brochures differ?

A business brochure leads with services, proof, and a clear contact panel, while an event brochure leads with the date, venue, schedule, and registration. Both use the same fold and panel structure, but the order of importance flips: business brochures sell capability over time, and event brochures drive a single time-bound action.

  • Business brochure — cover the offer, services, and credibility, then close with contact details. The reader keeps it for reference, so clarity and proof matter more than urgency.
  • Event brochure — front the what, when, and where, then the agenda and how to register. The reader acts once, so the date, venue, and registration link need the most visual weight.
  • Product brochure — show the product, its benefits, and specs, supported by strong imagery. Pair it with clean, consistent photography for each item.

How do you make a brochure look professional?

A professional brochure relies on visual hierarchy, generous white space, and consistent branding. Lead each panel with one bold headline, keep body text short and scannable, limit yourself to two or three fonts, and repeat your brand colors throughout. Restraint reads as polish, while clutter reads as amateur work.

  1. Set a clear hierarchy — one dominant headline, a supporting subhead, then body text on every panel
  2. Respect white space — leave breathing room around text and images so nothing feels cramped
  3. Limit fonts and colors — two or three typefaces and a tight palette keep the design cohesive
  4. Use high-quality images — sharp, on-brand visuals at full resolution, never stretched or pixelated
  5. Align everything — consistent margins and grid lines make the layout feel deliberate
  6. End with one call to action — tell the reader exactly what to do next, in one place

An AI brochure maker handles the alignment and spacing math automatically, but the editorial choices — what to emphasize and what to cut — stay yours. The cleaner your brief, the more polished the first draft.

What brochure mistakes should you avoid?

The most common brochure mistakes are cramming too much text onto every panel, ignoring the fold order, using low-resolution images, and forgetting the call to action. Each one quietly tells the reader the piece was rushed, and each is simple to fix once you know to look for it before exporting.

  • Wall-to-wall text — panels packed edge to edge overwhelm the reader; cut copy and add space
  • Ignoring the fold — content that crosses a fold line gets split awkwardly when folded
  • Low-resolution images — anything below 300 DPI looks blurry once it is printed
  • No clear next step — a brochure without a call to action leaves the reader nowhere to go
  • Inconsistent branding — mismatched colors and fonts across panels break the sense of one cohesive piece

Before you send anything to print, fold a paper proof or preview the panel order on screen. Seeing the brochure the way the reader will — one panel at a time, in fold sequence — catches layout problems that a flat view hides.
