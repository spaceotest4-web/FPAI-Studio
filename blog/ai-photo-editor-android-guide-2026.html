What changed in mobile photo editing

AI photo editing on Android in 2026 performs operations that required desktop Photoshop expertise three years ago — relighting a scene, reconstructing detail in low-resolution images, extending a photo beyond its original frame. The shift is not incremental filter improvement; it is a category change where the editor understands image content rather than just adjusting pixel values.

FP AI Studio bundles six AI editing tools in one Android app. This guide explains what each does, when to use it, and the order to chain them.

Tool 1 · AI upscaling (up to 4x)

AI upscaling increases image resolution while reconstructing detail — turning a 1000px image into 4000px without the blur of traditional resizing. The AI infers texture, edges, and fine detail from its training on millions of images.

Use it for: preparing images for print, rescuing low-resolution downloads, sharpening AI-generated images for product pages, enlarging old phone photos.

Skip it when: the source has heavy compression artifacts — upscale amplifies artifacts. Fix compression first or accept the ceiling. The full settings walkthrough is in the AI upscaling guide.

Tool 2 · AI relight

AI relight changes the lighting of a finished photo — direction, color temperature, intensity, and mood — as if you re-shot the scene with different lights. A flat midday photo becomes golden hour; a dim indoor shot becomes studio-lit.

Use it for: fixing badly lit portraits, matching product photos to a consistent catalog look, converting daytime shots to evening mood, adding drama to flat images.

Best practice: describe the target light in physical terms — "soft window light from the left, warm tone" outperforms "make it look better." Details in the AI relight guide.

Tool 3 · Background removal and replacement

AI background removal isolates the subject with clean edges — including hair, fur, and semi-transparent objects that defeated older tools. Replacement generates a new background that matches the subject's lighting and perspective.

Use it for: product photos on white, profile pictures with clean backdrops, compositing subjects into new scenes, marketplace listing photos.

The edge-quality test: zoom to the hairline after removal. 2026 models handle individual hair strands; if you see a halo, regenerate with a higher-quality source. Full workflow in the background removal guide.

Tool 4 · Style transfer

Style transfer re-renders your photo in the style of a reference image — watercolor, oil painting, anime, film stock, or any visual style you can show it. The content stays; the rendering changes.

Use it for: turning photos into art for printing, unifying a feed's visual identity, creating stylized versions of portraits, cartoon and anime conversions.

Best practice: the reference image should demonstrate the style clearly on a similar subject type. A watercolor landscape reference applied to a portrait produces weaker results than a watercolor portrait reference. Details in the style transfer guide.

Tool 5 · Image expand (outpainting)

Image expand extends a photo beyond its original frame — the AI generates what would plausibly exist outside the crop. A tight portrait becomes a wide environmental shot; a square photo becomes a 16:9 banner.

Use it for: converting aspect ratios without cropping (square to banner), recovering over-cropped shots, adding headroom for text overlays, creating wallpaper from phone photos.

Best practice: expand in steps (20-30 percent per pass) rather than doubling the canvas at once — quality holds better. Full guide at AI image expand.

Tool 6 · Old photo restoration

Restoration repairs damaged, faded, and low-quality old photos — removing scratches, recovering faded color, sharpening soft focus, and reconstructing damaged regions.

Use it for: family archive photos, scanned prints, water-damaged or torn photos, colorizing black-and-white images.

Expectation setting: restoration reconstructs plausibly, not forensically — a damaged face is rebuilt to look natural, which may shift likeness slightly. Keep originals. Full walkthrough in the photo restoration guide.

The right order to chain edits

When a photo needs multiple operations, order matters. The chain that preserves maximum quality:

  1. Restore first (if old/damaged) — every later step amplifies damage you leave in
  2. Background removal/replacement second — work on the subject before stylistic changes
  3. Relight third — match subject and new background lighting
  4. Style transfer fourth — stylistic rendering goes over the corrected base
  5. Expand fifth — extend the finished look outward
  6. Upscale last — always the final step; upscaling earlier wastes processing on pixels you may change

The complete pipeline with examples is in the complete AI editing workflow guide.
