AI photo retouching cleans up a portrait — evening out skin tone, clearing blemishes, and sculpting light — while keeping the pores, fine lines, and texture that make a face read as real. On Android, the whole process now takes a few taps and a few seconds. There is no desktop software to install and no frequency-separation layers to build by hand.

This guide explains how AI photo retouching actually works, walks through the natural-skin workflow in FP AI Studio, and covers the settings and habits that separate a believable portrait from a waxy, over-processed one.

The core idea: good retouching is subtraction, not blur. You remove the distractions — a stray blemish, an uneven patch, a shiny highlight — while leaving the skin's natural texture intact. The plastic look comes from erasing texture, so restraint matters more than strength.

What AI photo retouching does

AI photo retouching evens out skin tone, removes blemishes, and refines light on a face while preserving natural texture. Unlike a one-tap beauty filter that blurs everything uniformly, it targets specific flaws — a spot here, a shiny patch there — so the portrait looks cleaned up rather than smoothed into a mask.

That single capability replaces three older retouching chores:

  • Healing brush by hand — no more manually patching each blemish one at a time
  • Frequency separation on desktop — the texture-preserving result runs on your phone in seconds
  • Manual dodge and burn — the AI reads the contours of the face and shapes light automatically

How AI photo retouching works

AI photo retouching runs on facial analysis plus selective editing. The model first detects the face and segments it into regions — skin, eyes, lips, hair, brows — then applies smoothing and tone correction only to the skin while leaving everything else sharp. This is why a good retouch never blurs eyelashes or softens the edge of the lips.

Portrait retouching has become one of the most-used AI editing features of 2026, alongside relighting and background editing, according to industry coverage of this year's photo-editing trends. The pipeline behind a few taps looks like this:

  1. Detect — the model finds the face and its landmarks
  2. Segment — it separates skin from eyes, lips, hair, and brows
  3. Even out tone — blotchy and red patches are balanced toward the dominant skin color
  4. Remove blemishes — spots and marks are healed against surrounding skin
  5. Preserve texture — pores and fine detail are re-applied so the result is not waxy

Two details decide quality at this stage: how much smoothing you apply, and whether texture is preserved during the smooth. The best AI retouching tools smooth at a low frequency — flattening color blotches — while keeping the high-frequency texture that reads as real skin. That separation is what stops a portrait from looking plastic.

Retouch a portrait on Android: step by step

To retouch a portrait in FP AI Studio, open your photo, apply light skin smoothing, then spot-remove individual blemishes and adjust tone — the AI handles the heavy lifting in a few seconds. The full workflow takes under a minute for most portraits, and you can dial smoothing back down if the first pass looks too soft.

  1. Open your portrait in FP AI Studio and choose the retouching tool
  2. Apply light smoothing — start low and raise it only until tone evens out
  3. Spot-remove blemishes — tap individual spots with a small brush instead of blanket smoothing
  4. Refine tone — balance red or blotchy patches toward the surrounding skin
  5. Check texture at 100% — zoom in to confirm pores and fine detail survived
  6. Dial back if waxy — lower the smoothing strength if the skin looks plastic
  7. Export at full resolution once the portrait reads naturally

For a portrait that needs more than retouching — say, cleaning the skin and also fixing flat, even lighting — handle the skin first, then chain in AI relighting so the light shapes a face you have already cleaned up.

How do you smooth skin without the plastic look?

You smooth skin naturally by keeping the strength low, masking smoothing to skin only, and preserving texture instead of erasing it. The plastic look almost always comes from one mistake: pushing smoothing so high that pores disappear and the surface turns waxy. Restraint, not power, makes the difference.

  1. Start at low strength — raise smoothing only until blotches even out, then stop
  2. Spot-heal instead of blanket-smoothing — remove individual blemishes so the rest of the skin stays untouched
  3. Keep texture on — preserve pores and fine lines so the skin still reads as a photograph
  4. Mask to skin only — keep smoothing off eyes, lips, hair, and brows so they stay sharp
  5. Inspect at full size — a portrait that looks smooth on a thumbnail can look plastic at 100%
  6. Sharpen afterward if needed — restore crispness across the frame with the AI photo enhancer once the retouch is done

What is dodge and burn, and why does it matter?

Dodge and burn is selectively brightening (dodge) and darkening (burn) small areas of a portrait to even out skin tone and shape the face. It smooths blotchy patches and adds gentle dimension without blurring texture, which is why retouchers reach for it instead of heavy smoothing. AI retouching can apply it automatically by reading the contours of the face.

  • Even out blotches — burn down a bright shine, dodge up a dark shadow under the eyes
  • Add dimension — gentle contrast across the cheeks and jaw shapes the face naturally
  • Keep texture — because it changes brightness, not blur, pores and fine detail survive
  • Work locally — small, low-opacity passes beat one heavy adjustment over the whole face

The advantage of dodge and burn over smoothing is that it solves the same tone problems without sacrificing texture. A face evened out with light still looks like skin; a face flattened with smoothing starts to look like plastic. Used together — a little smoothing, a little dodge and burn — they keep portraits clean and believable.

Retouching by portrait type

The same retouching workflow adapts to the portrait types people clean up most: headshots, beauty and close-up shots, everyday selfies, and group photos. Each has a predictable fix to prioritize, and knowing it in advance speeds up the edit and keeps the result natural.

  • Professional headshots — even tone and a clean, confident face. Keep retouching subtle so the portrait still looks like the person; for a polished result pair it with the AI headshots and portraits workflow.
  • Beauty and close-ups — pores and fine detail are front and center, so texture preservation matters most. Spot-heal blemishes and dodge-and-burn tone rather than smoothing the whole face.
  • Everyday selfies — quick blemish removal and a touch of tone balancing. A light pass is usually all a selfie needs to look clean without looking edited.
  • Group photos — retouch each face individually so smoothing strength matches each person's skin instead of applying one blanket setting across the frame.

Whichever portrait you shoot, the rule holds: retouch the skin first, then handle global edits like lighting and color. The Android photo editor guide covers where each of these tools lives in the app.

Light vs medium vs heavy retouching

Light, medium, and heavy retouching suit different goals, and matching the level to the use keeps portraits believable. Light retouching evens tone and clears the odd blemish; medium adds dodge and burn and gentle smoothing; heavy reshapes and polishes for commercial work. Going heavier than the photo needs is what creates the plastic look.

LevelWhat it doesBest for
LightEven tone and remove a few blemishesSelfies and natural everyday portraits
MediumAdd dodge and burn plus gentle smoothingHeadshots and social profiles
HeavyFull skin polish and shapingBeauty, editorial, and commercial work

The three levels build on one another. A common sequence is a light pass to clear blemishes, a medium pass to even tone with dodge and burn, then a final check at full size to confirm texture survived before you commit to anything heavier.

AI retouching vs beauty filters

AI photo retouching beats one-tap beauty filters on three fronts: it targets specific flaws instead of blurring the whole face, it preserves skin texture rather than flattening it, and it gives you control over strength. Beauty filters are fast and fun for a quick share; a proper retouching tool is what you reach for when the portrait needs to look real.

  • Targeted vs blanket — retouching heals individual blemishes, while a filter smooths everything uniformly, eyes and lips included.
  • Texture preservation — retouching keeps pores and fine detail, whereas heavy filters wipe them out and leave a waxy surface.
  • Control over strength — you choose how much to smooth and where, instead of accepting a single fixed filter intensity.
  • Full-resolution output — a dedicated editor exports at the original resolution rather than a downscaled share size.

The practical rule: use a beauty filter for a quick, casual post, and reach for AI photo retouching when the portrait matters — a headshot, a profile photo, or any image where the goal is a clean face that still looks like skin.

When AI photo retouching struggles

AI photo retouching struggles with very low-resolution images, heavy makeup or face paint, and unusual lighting that confuses skin detection. The less clearly the model can read the face, the more it has to guess — and guessed detail is where the result starts to look off.

  • Low-resolution photos — little texture to preserve, so smoothing has nothing real to keep
  • Heavy makeup or face paint — the model can mistake painted patterns for skin tone
  • Harsh or colored lighting — strong color casts confuse tone correction
  • Partly hidden faces — hair, hands, or angles that obscure the skin make segmentation unreliable

When a portrait is too difficult for one pass, work in stages: even the tone first, retouch the clearest areas of skin, then handle the tricky regions against the cleaner face you just created.

FAQ

Is AI photo retouching free to use?

FP AI Studio includes retouching in its Android photo editor, and most edits are free to produce. You smooth skin, clear blemishes, and adjust tone, then export the result. Free tiers across AI editors typically cap resolution or daily edits, so check the limit if you retouch large batches or need full-resolution portrait exports.

How do I keep skin texture when retouching with AI?

Use a low smoothing strength and apply it only to even out tone, not to erase every pore. Remove individual blemishes with a small spot tool rather than blanket smoothing the whole face. Pores, fine lines, and stubble are what make skin read as real, so retouch lightly and check the result at full size before exporting.

What is dodge and burn in photo retouching?

Dodge and burn is selectively brightening (dodge) and darkening (burn) small areas of a portrait to even out skin tone and shape the face. It smooths blotchy patches and adds gentle dimension without blurring texture. AI retouching can apply dodge and burn automatically by reading the contours of the face, which is far faster than doing it by hand.

Why do AI-retouched portraits sometimes look plastic?

The plastic look comes from over-smoothing — pushing skin smoothing so high that pores and fine detail vanish, leaving a waxy surface. It also happens when smoothing spills onto hair, eyes, and lips. Keep the strength low, mask smoothing to skin only, and preserve texture so the portrait still looks like a photograph rather than a render.

What is the difference between retouching and editing a photo?

Retouching cleans up the subject — skin, blemishes, stray hairs, and tone — while editing usually refers to global changes like exposure, color, and cropping. Retouching is local and subject-focused; editing is broad and scene-wide. Most portrait workflows do both, retouching the face first and then applying global edits like lighting and color afterward.
