The core idea: a great X header is composition first, art second. The avatar covers the lower-left and the profile name sits over the bottom edge, so the readable part of a 1500x500 banner is really the top and right two-thirds. Design for that zone and the rest follows.

What size is an X header in 2026?

The X header is 1500x500 pixels at a 3:1 aspect ratio, exported as JPG or PNG under 5MB. That has been the standard banner size since the platform rebranded from Twitter, and it has not changed in 2026. Design at the full 1500x500 so the banner stays sharp on large displays and downscales cleanly on phones.

A few details decide whether your export looks crisp or cropped:

  1. Canvas — build at exactly 1500x500 px, never a smaller size scaled up
  2. Aspect ratio — 3:1; X will letterbox or crop anything that does not match
  3. File type — JPG for photographic banners, PNG for flat color and crisp text
  4. File size — keep it under 5MB so the upload is not recompressed harder than needed
  5. Responsive crop — X trims the top and bottom edges on narrow screens, so keep key content centered vertically

The banner displays at different heights across desktop, the mobile app, and the embedded preview, so treat the outer edges as flexible and the middle band as the part guaranteed to show. A practical rule is to design for the worst-case crop: imagine the top and bottom 60 pixels disappearing on a narrow phone and a slim profile preview, and place nothing you cannot afford to lose in those strips. If a generated banner already looks balanced when you mentally shave those edges, it will hold up everywhere X shows it.

Where does the avatar overlap the header?

The circular avatar sits in the lower-left of the header and covers roughly the bottom-left fifth of the banner. On a 1500x500 design, keep the leftmost 250 to 300 pixels and the bottom 120 pixels clear of text or logos, because the avatar, display name, and handle render over that zone on nearly every layout.

Picture the 1500x500 canvas split into three working areas, and you can place every element with confidence:

The single most common mistake is centering a logo or slogan, where the avatar then slices into it. Anchor your text and brand mark in the upper area or the right side, and the avatar becomes part of the composition rather than an obstacle. A useful trick is to treat the avatar as a deliberate anchor point: if the banner art flows around an open circle in the lower-left, the avatar drops in like a missing puzzle piece instead of fighting the background. Personal brand or business: what changes?

A personal-brand header leans on a clear focal subject, a short tagline, and a consistent palette that matches your avatar, while a business header foregrounds the logo, a value proposition, and a single call to action. The 1500x500 spec and safe zones are identical; the brief and hierarchy differ.

  • Personal brand — one short line that says what you do, a palette that echoes your avatar, and breathing room. The goal is recognition, so keep it simple and repeat the same colors across posts.
  • Business or product — logo placed in the upper-right safe zone, a one-line value proposition, and an optional launch or campaign tag. Treat the banner like the top of a landing page.
  • Creator or community — a recurring motif or character, with space reserved for a rotating tagline you can swap each season without redesigning the whole banner.

When the header is part of a paid push or product launch, the same composition discipline applies as in any promotional visual — anchor the message, keep one call to action, and avoid clutter. How do you get an on-brand, readable banner?

An on-brand X header comes down to three habits: respect the avatar and name safe zones, keep contrast high enough that any text survives compression, and reuse one palette across your whole profile. Most weak banners fail on layout and legibility, not on the quality of the art itself.

  1. Reserve the lower-left — leave the avatar zone open so nothing important gets covered
  2. Push text to the top-right — the most reliably visible band across devices
  3. Hold high contrast — light text on a dark area, or the reverse, so it reads after X recompresses the upload
  4. Limit the palette — two or three brand colors keep the banner cohesive with your avatar and posts
  5. Test the mobile crop — preview on a phone, where the top and bottom edges trim
  6. Generate options — compare a few variations and pick the strongest composition, not just the prettiest art

Header, avatar, or post graphic: which do you need?

The header, the avatar, and a post graphic are three different X assets that are easy to confuse. The header is the wide 1500x500 banner, the avatar is the small circular profile picture that overlaps it, and a post graphic is a square or 16:9 image shared in the feed. Pick the asset that matches where it appears.

The three work as a set. A strong profile generates the header and avatar from one palette so they sit together, then carries that palette into post graphics, so a visitor scrolling the feed recognizes the brand before they read the name.

When does an AI header miss?

An AI Twitter header generator struggles most with crisp small text, exact logo reproduction, and tight composition around the avatar zone. The art is rarely the problem; the misses cluster around legibility and layout, both of which a quick refine or a manual text pass can fix.

  • Fine text rendering — generated lettering can warp, so add critical text and logos as a clean overlay rather than baking them into the prompt
  • Exact brand logos — the model approximates marks; place your real logo on top for accuracy
  • Avatar collisions — busy lower-left detail clashes with the circular avatar; regenerate or reframe
  • Over-recompression — low-contrast text can muddy after X processes the upload, so keep contrast generous

The practical rule: let the generator handle the background, the mood, and the composition, then add your exact logo and any must-read text as a final layer over the safe zone. That split gives you the speed of generation with the precision of a designed banner. It also keeps your banner easy to update later, since swapping a tagline or a campaign line on a clean overlay takes seconds and never forces you to regenerate the underlying art. Once the system is set, a new header for a launch, a season, or a rebrand is a small edit rather than a project.

FAQ

What is the correct Twitter header size in 2026?

The Twitter, now X, header is 1500x500 pixels at a 3:1 aspect ratio. Export at that resolution as JPG or PNG and keep the file under 5MB. X resizes the banner to fit different screens, so design at full 1500x500 and keep anything important away from the edges that get cropped on mobile.

How do I keep my X header text from being hidden?

Place text in the upper area and the right two-thirds of the 1500x500 banner, away from the avatar in the lower-left. X overlays the avatar, display name, and sometimes a join-date line over the lower portion, so headline text and logos read most reliably in the top-right safe zone.

What is the difference between a Twitter header and a profile picture?

The header is the wide 1500x500 banner across the top of your profile. The profile picture is the small circular avatar that overlaps the header in the lower-left, displayed at 400x400. The header sets the scene and brand; the avatar identifies you. Design them together so the avatar does not clash with the banner behind it.
