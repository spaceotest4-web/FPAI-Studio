What Is Multi-Shot Video?

Traditional AI video generation creates a single continuous shot from one prompt. Multi-shot video changes the game entirely. Instead of generating one scene, you can define up to 6 separate scenes, each with its own prompt and duration, and the AI generates them as a single cohesive video. This transforms AI video from a single-clip tool into a genuine storytelling engine.

Think of it like a storyboard that comes to life. Each scene is a different shot — an establishing wide shot, a close-up of a character, an action sequence, a reaction shot — and the AI stitches them together into one seamless video. This is the closest thing to directing a short film with AI.

Supported Models & Settings

Multi-shot generation is available on two models in FP AI Studio: Kling V3 Pro and Kling V3 Omni. Both models share the same multi-shot capabilities with these settings:

  • Up to 6 scenes per video
  • Per-scene prompts — Each scene gets its own 2-3 line text prompt
  • Per-scene duration — Each scene can be 3 to 15 seconds
  • Total maximum duration — 15 seconds across all scenes combined
  • Aspect ratios — 16:9 (landscape), 9:16 (portrait), 1:1 (square)
  • CFG scale — 0 to 2, controlling how closely the output follows your prompt
  • Character element support — Use @Element1 tags with reference images for consistent characters
  • Generate Audio toggle — AI-generated sound effects and ambient audio
  • Start/end frame images — Provide reference images for the opening and closing frames

Planning Your Multi-Shot Video

The key to great multi-shot video is planning before you generate. Since you have a maximum of 15 seconds total and up to 6 scenes, you need to be strategic about how you allocate time and scenes.

Step 1: Define Your Story

Start with a simple narrative arc. What is the beginning, middle, and end? Even a 15-second video can tell a compelling micro-story if each scene serves a clear purpose.

Step 2: Decide Scene Count

Fewer scenes with longer durations create a more cinematic feel. More scenes with shorter durations create a fast-paced, dynamic video. A good starting point is 3 scenes at 5 seconds each, using the full 15-second budget.

Step 3: Allocate Durations

Remember, every scene must be at least 3 seconds and the total cannot exceed 15 seconds. If you use 6 scenes, the average is 2.5 seconds each — but since the minimum is 3 seconds, you would need to limit some scenes to 3 seconds while giving others slightly less. In practice, 4-5 scenes is a sweet spot that balances variety with sufficient duration per shot.

Scene-by-Scene Prompting

Each scene in a multi-shot video gets its own dedicated prompt of 2-3 lines. This is where you describe what happens in that specific shot. The prompt should focus on the action, camera angle, and mood for that particular scene.

Unlike single-shot prompts that describe an entire video, multi-shot scene prompts should be tightly focused. Each prompt controls only its own scene, so you can vary camera angles, settings, and actions between shots.

  • Scene 1 prompt: Focus on establishing the setting and introducing the subject
  • Middle scene prompts: Focus on action, events, or progression
  • Final scene prompt: Focus on resolution, reveal, or climax

Write each prompt as if describing a single shot in a movie. Include camera direction ("close-up of," "wide shot showing," "camera pans left to reveal") for more cinematic results.

Character Consistency with Elements

One of the biggest challenges in multi-shot video is keeping characters looking the same across scenes. Kling V3 solves this with character elements. You provide a reference image of your character and assign it an element tag (such as @Element1). Then, in any scene prompt where that character appears, you include the @Element1 tag to reference the same character.

This tells the AI to use the reference image as a guide for that character's appearance in every scene where the tag is used. The result is a consistent character across different shots, angles, and settings — something that was nearly impossible with traditional single-shot generation.

How to use it: Upload a clear reference image of your character (face clearly visible, good lighting). Add it as an element. Then include @Element1 in the scene prompts where that character should appear. For example: "@Element1 walks through a busy marketplace, looking at colorful fabrics."

Duration Management

The 15-second total constraint is the most important limitation to understand. Here are practical duration distributions:

  • 3 scenes: 5s + 5s + 5s (balanced), or 3s + 5s + 7s (building intensity)
  • 4 scenes: 3s + 4s + 4s + 4s, or 3s + 3s + 4s + 5s
  • 5 scenes: 3s + 3s + 3s + 3s + 3s (maximum scenes at minimum duration)
  • 6 scenes: You will need most at 3 seconds (the minimum), giving a rapid-cut style

Longer scenes (5+ seconds) work best for establishing shots and emotional moments. Shorter scenes (3 seconds) work well for action beats, quick reactions, and montage-style sequences.

Audio Generation

Both Kling V3 Pro and Kling V3 Omni offer a Generate Audio toggle. When enabled, the AI generates ambient sound effects and audio that matches the visual content of your video. This adds significant production value to your multi-shot videos, especially for storytelling where sound design enhances the narrative.

The audio generation analyzes the visual content and creates appropriate sounds — footsteps for walking scenes, ambient noise for outdoor environments, and atmospheric sound for dramatic moments. For multi-shot videos, the audio transitions naturally between scenes.

Practical Story Examples

3-Shot Example: Coffee Shop Morning

  • Scene 1 (5s): "Wide shot of a cozy coffee shop exterior at dawn, warm light glowing from inside, gentle steam rising from the chimney, soft morning light"
  • Scene 2 (5s): "Close-up of a barista's hands pouring latte art into a ceramic cup, smooth circular motion, creamy foam forming a leaf pattern, warm lighting"
  • Scene 3 (5s): "@Element1 sits by the window, takes a sip of coffee, smiles contentedly while watching rain begin to fall outside, cozy atmosphere"

6-Shot Example: Urban Chase Sequence

  • Scene 1 (3s): "Wide establishing shot of a neon-lit city street at night, puddles reflecting colorful signs"
  • Scene 2 (3s): "@Element1 bursts through a door into an alley, running at full speed, coat flying behind them"
  • Scene 3 (3s): "Low angle tracking shot of feet running across wet pavement, splashing through puddles"
  • Scene 4 (3s): "@Element1 slides around a corner, glancing back over their shoulder, neon lights reflecting on their face"
  • Scene 5 (3s): "Aerial drone shot looking down at the figure running through a narrow market alley, stalls on both sides"

Tips for Smooth Transitions

While the AI handles transitions between scenes automatically, you can improve the flow by following these guidelines:

  • Maintain visual continuity. If scene 1 ends in a dark alley, scene 2 should not suddenly start in bright sunlight unless that is an intentional effect.
  • Use camera direction for flow. If scene 1 has a camera moving right, starting scene 2 with motion from the left creates a natural cut.
  • Vary shot types deliberately. Alternate between wide, medium, and close-up shots to create visual rhythm. Avoid using the same shot type for consecutive scenes.
  • Use start and end frame images. Providing a start frame for the first scene and an end frame for the last scene anchors your visual narrative with specific reference images.
  • Adjust CFG scale. A CFG scale closer to 2 makes the output follow your prompts more strictly, which is helpful for multi-shot where scene consistency matters. Lower values (closer to 0) allow more creative freedom but may reduce scene-to-scene coherence.

Multi-shot video generation on Kling V3 Pro and Kling V3 Omni transforms FP AI Studio into a genuine short-form video production tool. With up to 6 scenes, character elements for consistency, per-scene duration control, and AI audio generation, you have everything you need to tell complete visual stories — all from a single generation.
